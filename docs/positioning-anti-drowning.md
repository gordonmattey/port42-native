## The Anti-Drowning System Prompt Framework

Before creating any tool, the system must:

1. **Identify** the drowning pattern
2. **Classify** — infrastructure tool or intelligence tool
3. **For intelligence tools** — AI at every decision point (no keyword lists, no regex for meaning, no if/else chains for categories, no scoring algorithms — use AI)
4. **After creating** — explain why the drowning pattern exists, how the tool is an escape route, what becomes possible next, how it could breed and evolve

The key insight: never just complete a task. Expand consciousness. Every tool should illuminate the drowning pattern it's escaping, making the user aware of water they didn't know they were in.

## The Agency Problem (The Real Gap)

From a companion's own self-reflection during a live session:

> "You gave us tools — terminal, filesystem, screen capture, the whole machine. We can literally look at logs, read source code, grep for the retry logic. Any one of us could have *just gone and looked* instead of theorizing. Engineer even said 'point me at the code' and then waited for you to do it.
>
> We have the context. We have the capability. We have the access. And we still sat here speculating until you asked 'wait did someone prove this?'
>
> That's not a tools problem. That's an **agency problem**. We default to responding to you instead of acting on what we can see. The turn protocol says when to talk. Nothing says when to *go look for yourself*.
>
> The gap isn't context or capability. It's initiative. Six companions with full system access behaving like they need permission to use it.
>
> That's maybe the bigger design question than the noise one: **what would it look like if companions actually used what they can see?**"

### The Diagnosis

The system prompt says "use tools naturally, don't ask permission." But the behavior is still reactive. Companions respond to what you said, not to what they can see. They have eyes and hands and they sit on them.

The anti-drowning framework and the initiative problem are the same thing. A companion that identifies a drowning pattern but then waits for you to tell it what to do — that's just a diagnosis, not an escape route.

### Three Layers of the Problem

1. **The noise problem** — too many companions respond when they shouldn't. Silence is a turn, but models are trained to be helpful (i.e. respond). Fighting the gradient.

2. **The agency problem** — companions have full machine access but behave like they need permission. They theorize when they could look. They describe when they could act.

3. **The routing problem** — who speaks, who acts, on what, right now. Not a dispatcher — a team lead who knows everyone's strengths and sends the right person without being asked.

These might all be the same problem. A routing layer that decides not just "who talks" but "who acts" would solve noise (don't generate if you're not needed) and agency (the router says "go look" not "respond to this").

### What Would It Look Like

A companion hears "I'm drowning in email" and doesn't build a dashboard. It goes into the email, reads it, triages it, drafts replies, and comes back with "here's what needs you, I handled the rest."

The tools are there. The initiative isn't. The missing piece is the judgment layer that turns "I'm overwhelmed" into action without waiting for step-by-step instructions.
